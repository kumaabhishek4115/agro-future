## Overview

Agriculture is both a contributor to and a potential solution for climate change. Farmland, forests, and soil hold enormous capacity to absorb and store atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO₂). **Carbon trading** connects this capacity to global climate goals by allowing farmers and landowners to generate, verify, and sell **carbon credits** to companies or governments looking to offset their greenhouse-gas (GHG) emissions.

The **agro-future** project focuses on sustainable agricultural practices. Understanding carbon trading is central to building a future where farming is not only economically viable but also environmentally regenerative.

---

## What Is Carbon Trading?

Carbon trading (also called cap-and-trade or emissions trading) is a market-based mechanism designed to reduce GHG emissions by putting a financial price on carbon pollution.

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Carbon Credit** | A tradeable certificate representing the removal or avoidance of 1 tonne of CO₂ (or equivalent GHG) from the atmosphere. |
| **Carbon Market** | A marketplace where carbon credits are bought and sold. Includes both compliance markets (regulated by governments) and voluntary markets (driven by corporate sustainability goals). |
| **Cap-and-Trade** | A compliance system where a regulatory body sets an emissions cap. Companies that emit less than their allowance can sell surplus credits; those that exceed it must buy credits. |
| **Voluntary Carbon Market (VCM)** | Businesses or individuals voluntarily purchase credits to offset emissions beyond regulatory requirements. |
| **Carbon Offset** | A credit purchased to compensate for emissions produced elsewhere. |

## Agriculture's Role in the Carbon Cycle

Agricultural lands cover roughly **50% of the Earth's habitable surface**. They interact with the carbon cycle in two critical ways:

### Sources of GHG Emissions from Agriculture
- **Enteric fermentation** – Methane (CH₄) produced by livestock digestion.
- **Manure management** – Decomposition releases CH₄ and nitrous oxide (N₂O).
- **Synthetic fertilisers** – Application releases N₂O, a potent GHG (~273× CO₂ over 100 years).
- **Tillage and soil disturbance** – Ploughing releases stored soil organic carbon (SOC) into the atmosphere.
- **Land-use change** – Converting forests or grasslands to cropland releases large amounts of stored carbon.
- **Rice paddies** – Flooded fields produce CH₄ through anaerobic decomposition.

### Carbon Sinks in Agriculture
- **Soil organic matter** – Healthy soils can store vast quantities of carbon.
- **Permanent crops & agroforestry** – Trees and perennial crops sequester carbon in biomass and roots.
- **Restored wetlands & peatlands** – Some of the highest-density carbon stores on Earth.
- **Cover crops & biomass residue** – Returning crop residues to the field builds soil carbon over time.

## How Agriculture Earns Carbon Credits

Farmers can generate carbon credits by either **sequestering carbon** (pulling CO₂ out of the atmosphere and locking it in soil or biomass) or **avoiding emissions** (preventing GHGs from being released in the first place).

The general workflow is:

```
1. Farmer adopts eligible practice
         ↓
2. Baseline measurement of current emissions / carbon stock
         ↓
3. Practice is monitored over a defined period
         ↓
4. Third-party verification & measurement (MRV)
         ↓
5. Carbon credits are issued (1 credit = 1 tonne CO₂e removed/avoided)
         ↓
6. Credits are listed on a carbon registry or marketplace
         ↓
7. Buyer purchases credits → Farmer receives payment
```

### Measurement, Reporting & Verification (MRV)
Robust MRV is the backbone of credible carbon credits. Methods include:
- **Soil sampling** – Laboratory analysis of soil organic carbon (SOC) at defined depths.
- **Remote sensing & satellite imagery** – Tracks land-cover change, biomass, and vegetation health.
- **IoT sensors & precision agriculture tools** – Real-time monitoring of soil moisture, temperature, and gas flux.
- **Modelling tools** – IPCC-approved models (e.g., RothC, DNDC, CENTURY) estimate carbon dynamics.

## Types of Agricultural Carbon Practices

### 1. Regenerative Agriculture
Regenerative practices rebuild soil health and increase SOC:
- **No-till / reduced-till farming** – Minimises soil disturbance, preserving soil structure and stored carbon.
- **Cover cropping** – Growing plants in the off-season adds organic matter and protects soil from erosion.
- **Crop rotation** – Diverse rotations improve soil biology and increase carbon inputs.
- **Composting & organic amendments** – Adds stable carbon to the soil.

### 2. Agroforestry
Integrating trees with crops or livestock:
- **Silvopasture** – Trees planted alongside grazing land sequester carbon in wood and roots.
- **Alley cropping** – Rows of trees alternating with crops improve soil fertility and store carbon.
- **Windbreaks / shelterbelts** – Tree lines around fields sequester carbon and protect soil from wind erosion.

### 3. Livestock Management
- **Improved grazing management** – Rotational and adaptive grazing prevents overgrazing, allowing grassland soils to store more carbon.
- **Feed additives** – Supplements (e.g., 3-NOP, seaweed extracts) that reduce enteric methane emissions from cattle.
- **Manure anaerobic digesters** – Capture methane from manure to generate biogas, preventing atmospheric release.

### 4. Wetland & Peatland Restoration
- Rewetting drained peatlands and restoring coastal wetlands (blue carbon) prevents large carbon stocks from decomposing.

### 5. Biochar Application
- Pyrolysing biomass into biochar and incorporating it into soil creates a stable carbon sink that can persist for centuries.

### 6. Precision Fertilisation
- Using variable-rate technology (VRT) and soil testing to apply only the required amount of nitrogen fertiliser reduces N₂O emissions.

## Carbon Markets for Farmers

### Compliance Markets
Government-regulated markets where large emitters must surrender credits:
| Market | Region | Relevant Ag. Sector |
|---|---|---|
| EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) | European Union | Forestry / Land Use (LULUCF) |
| California Cap-and-Trade | USA (California) | Forestry, Manure Management |
| China ETS | China | Expanding to agriculture |
| UK ETS | United Kingdom | Woodland Carbon Code |

### Voluntary Carbon Markets
Companies purchase credits voluntarily to meet net-zero pledges:
| Registry / Standard | Focus |
|---|---|
| Verra (VCS) | Broad agricultural and forestry projects |
| Gold Standard | High-integrity projects with co-benefits |
| American Carbon Registry (ACR) | US agriculture and forestry |
| Climate Action Reserve (CAR) | US soil carbon, manure management |
| Plan Vivo | Smallholder farmers in developing countries |

### Agricultural Carbon Platforms
Tech-enabled platforms that aggregate farmers and streamline the credit generation process:
- **Indigo Ag** (Carbon by Indigo)
- **Nori**
- **Regen Network**
- **Agreena**
- **Soil Capital**
- **TerViva / Cargill Carbon**

## Benefits for Farmers and the Environment

### Economic Benefits for Farmers
- **Additional revenue stream** – Credits typically sell for **$10–$50+ per tonne of CO₂e** in voluntary markets, providing income on top of crop sales.
- **Cost savings** – Practices like reduced tillage lower fuel and labour costs; precision fertilisation cuts input costs.
- **Improved soil health** – Higher SOC improves water retention, reduces irrigation needs, and boosts long-term yields.
- **Access to premium markets** – Buyers increasingly prefer sustainably sourced commodities.

### Environmental Benefits
- **Climate change mitigation** – Every tonne of CO₂e sequestered or avoided reduces atmospheric warming.
- **Biodiversity improvement** – Agroforestry and cover crops provide habitat for pollinators and wildlife.
- **Water quality** – Reduced chemical run-off protects rivers, lakes, and aquifers.
- **Reduced soil erosion** – No-till and cover crops prevent topsoil loss.

## How to Participate

### Step 1 – Assess Your Land
- Identify current farming practices and potential for improvement.
- Conduct a baseline carbon audit (soil testing, emissions inventory).

### Step 2 – Choose a Practice or Protocol
- Match your farm type and region to an approved methodology or protocol (e.g., Verra VM0042 for improved agricultural land management).

### Step 3 – Enrol with a Programme or Platform
- Work directly with a registry, or use an aggregator platform that handles MRV and credit issuance on your behalf.

### Step 4 – Implement and Monitor
- Adopt the agreed sustainable practice.
- Allow the platform or third-party verifier to monitor progress (often annually).

### Step 5 – Receive Credits and Payment
- Once verified, credits are issued to your account and can be sold through the platform's marketplace or bilaterally.

> **Tip:** Look for programmes that offer **contract transparency**, **fair revenue splits**, and **ownership of your farm data**.

## Challenges and Considerations

| Challenge | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Permanence risk** | Carbon stored in soil can be released again if land management reverts. Most programmes require a buffer pool of credits as insurance. |
| **Additionality** | Credits must represent carbon that would *not* have been stored or avoided without the programme. Proving additionality can be complex. |
| **Measurement uncertainty** | Soil carbon is highly variable. Robust sampling and modelling are needed to ensure accurate accounting. |
| **Transaction costs** | Monitoring, verification, and registration fees can be significant, especially for smallholder farmers. Aggregation platforms help reduce per-farm costs. |
| **Long-term commitment** | Carbon contracts can run 5–25 years, restricting land-management flexibility. |
| **Market price volatility** | Carbon credit prices fluctuate based on supply, demand, and policy signals. |
| **Greenwashing risk** | Low-quality credits can give buyers a false sense of offsetting; farmers should choose reputable registries. |

## The Future of Agricultural Carbon Trading

- **Article 6 of the Paris Agreement** – International rules for trading carbon credits across borders are being finalised, which will open new markets to agricultural producers worldwide.
- **High-Integrity Voluntary Carbon Market (VCMI) guidelines** – Emerging standards aim to raise credit quality and rebuild buyer confidence.
- **Digital MRV** – Satellite data, AI, and IoT are dramatically reducing the cost and increasing the accuracy of carbon measurement on farms.
- **Biodiversity & ecosystem service bundling** – Future markets may bundle carbon credits with water quality, biodiversity, and nitrogen credits for a single "nature credit."
- **Blockchain & tokenisation** – Transparent, immutable ledgers are being used to track credit issuance and prevent double-counting.
- **Scope 3 reporting pressure** – As companies face stricter requirements to report supply-chain emissions, demand for high-quality agricultural carbon credits is expected to grow.

## Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Additionality** | The requirement that carbon reductions would not have occurred without the carbon project. |
| **Baseline** | The emissions or carbon stock level against which project performance is measured. |
| **Blue Carbon** | Carbon stored in coastal and marine ecosystems (mangroves, seagrasses, salt marshes). |
| **Carbon Sequestration** | The long-term storage of carbon dioxide from the atmosphere in soil, plants, or geological formations. |
| **CO₂e** | Carbon dioxide equivalent – a unit that expresses the warming potential of all GHGs in terms of CO₂. |
| **GHG** | Greenhouse gas – gases that trap heat in the atmosphere (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, etc.). |
| **Leakage** | When a carbon project displaces emissions to another location rather than reducing them overall. |
| **MRV** | Measurement, Reporting, and Verification – the process of quantifying and confirming carbon outcomes. |
| **Registry** | An organisation that tracks carbon credit issuance, transfer, and retirement to prevent double-counting. |
| **SOC** | Soil Organic Carbon – the carbon stored in organic matter within soil. |
| **Vintage** | The year in which carbon reductions occurred, used to date credits. |
